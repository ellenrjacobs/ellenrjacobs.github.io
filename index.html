About

Hello! My name is Ellen and I am a 3rd year PhD student in the Department of Biology at Georgetown University. My pronouns are she/her. https://orcid.org/0000-0003-2957-3286

What I do

I study the behavioral ecology of bottlenose dolphins. I’m interested in the interactions between individual behavior, social dynamics, and foraging ecology. My work uses a long-term dataset of behavioral observations of Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins in Shark Bay, Western Australia. Across the ocean, I’m also working on a large-scale passive acoustic monitoring project of the common bottlenose dolphins that use the Potomac River in Chesapeake Bay watershed on the East Coast of the United States.

Where I work

I am currently at Georgetown University in Washington, DC, USA, affiliated with both the Shark Bay Dolphin Research Project and the Potomac-Chesapeake Dolphin Research Project. Previously, I received my Masters of Science from Aarhus University in Aarhus Denmark, where I completed a thesis investigating the active space of sperm whale social calls in the Eastern Caribbean. Before graduate school, after completing my Bachelors of Science in Ecology, Behavior, and Evolution at the University of California, San Diego, I worked at the Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute, the Woods Hole Institute of Oceanography, and the Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute.

Current Projects

Maternal Style

I’m interested in the differences in style between individual mother dolphins in the resident population of bottlenose dolphins in Shark Bay, Western Australia.

Variation in individual maternal style has been established in many primate species but rarely in cetaceans. As a social species with a long period of calf dependence, bottlenose dolphins are an excellent system in which to investigate whether mothers show variation in maternal style. In this project, we leverage 30+ years of study of Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins (Tursiops aduncus) in Shark Bay, Australia to uncover whether individual differences in maternal style are consistent over multiple calves. Using population survey data (n=19587) and focal follow data (n=343) to extract behavioral observations, we examine characteristics (network position, activity budget, maternal care) of 16 mothers of 47 calves <2 years old. Using GLMMs, we show that maternal identity has a significant impact on all measures across multiple calves. Our approach indicates that maternal style is an individually consistent multidimensional trait and suggests diverse styles can achieve similar fitness outcomes, providing insight into processes shaping calf development.

PAM in the Potomac

I’m interested in understanding the way that bottlenose dolphins are using the Potomac River and how they may be affecting commercial fisheries in the area with their foraging behavior.

Since 2015, the Potomac-Chesapeake Dolphin Project has documented >1200 bottlenose dolphins in the Potomac River. However, the movement patterns of these animals within the Potomac River are poorly known. With a 100-year data gap in reported bottlenose dolphin sightings in the river, it is unclear whether their presence is a new phenomenon, an increase due to cleaner waters, or they have been here all along. In order to understand how bottlenose dolphins are currently using the river, we have deployed seven C-POD hydrophones spanning the lower tidal Potomac, from Dahlgren, Virginia to where the Potomac empties into the Chesapeake Bay. We will track dolphin movement within the river using the presence/absence of dolphin clicks recorded at each hydrophone site. From this, we can determine diel- and seasonal-scale movement patterns as well as patterns in foraging behavior. Bottlenose dolphins are key indicators of ecosystem health and water quality, so understanding their presence and role within the ecosystem will present an opportunity to cultivate a responsible relationship with these inhabitants of our aquatic ecosystems.
